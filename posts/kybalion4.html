"Mind (as well as metals and elements) may be transmuted, from state to state; degree to degree; condition to condition; pole to pole; vibration to vibration. True Hermetic Transmutation is a Mental Art."--The Kybalion.

But it must not be supposed that the ancients were ignorant of that which the modern schools suppose to be their exclusive and special property. Look really carefully at what happened there. In two cases he's describing simple history: people in the past knew astronomy and chemistry. Yes. Obviously.

Then he parallels those two statements to claim the ancients were doing psychology, especially the Egyptians. He doesn't say how, or what they were doing, or what the point was. What he's doing overall is trying to claim that magic is basically fancy psychology (when it's not superstition), and that this magic is... thinking really hard about things.


"Transmutation" is a term usually employed to designate the ancient art of the transmutation of metals--particularly of the base metals into gold. This is literally slapping "mental" onto a thing and behaving as though he's talking about something profound. Mental swimming is like swimming, but mental. But you can see where he's going in the next bit.

The first of the Seven Hermetic Principles is the Principle of Mentalism, the axiom of which is "THE ALL is Mind; the Universe is Mental," which means that the Underlying Reality of the Universe is Mind; and the Universe itself is Mental--that is, "existing in the Mind of THE ALL." We shall consider this Principle in succeeding lessons, but let us see the effect of the principle if it be assumed to be true.

If the Universe is Mental in its nature, then Mental Transmutation must be the art of CHANGING THE CONDITIONS OF THE UNIVERSE, along the lines of Matter, Force and mind. There's a BIG IF there WWA.

So, first, there's a freshman-level solipsism happening here. If the universe exists in the thoughts of a God, then it does not necessarily follow that the universe is -- and is only -- a mental state.

My cat exists in my mind, I think of him often. But he is not only my thought about him. The solipsism is in basically ignoring the autonomy of everything not the reader, as we'll see as we go.

Second, and more importantly, WWA is never going to prove this if statement. He's relying on it to make further claims, basically begging the question. If the universe is all mental, and if people can sit down and think really hard and control their mental states, then they can sit down and think really hard and control "material conditions."

Except even then this doesn't follow, because nothing in these assumptions, even if we did give them to WWA, demonstrates that changing mental states in oneself is the same as changing mental states in someone else, and "the All" is necessarily "someone else," even if we're part of it. But for the solipsism at the core of The Kybalion, nothing else really exists except the nebulous "All" and the reader.

Chapter IV: The All.

"Under, and back of, the Universe of Time, Space and Change, is ever to be found The Substantial Reality--the Fundamental Truth."--The Kybalion.

"Substance" means: "that which underlies all outward manifestations; the essence; the essential reality; the thing in itself," etc. "Substantial" means: "actually existing; being the essential element; being real," etc. "Reality" means: "the state of being real; true, enduring; valid; fixed; permanent; actual," etc.


This is WWA doing Kant's *ding-an-sich*, the Thing in Itself. But badly.

English professors in training learn just enough philosophy to be dangerous, so I'm not an expert here, but essentially, Kant divided away the "phenomena" of a thing, which is to say what we observe of it, from its essence, the thing in itself. And that is not universally acceptable in philosophy, for what it's worth. But yeah. You know. Philosophy! Sure! Maybe WWA is going to add something of substance to the centuries of debate about Kant's ideas!


All thinkers, in all lands and in all times, have assumed the necessity for postulating the existence of this Substantial Reality. All philosophies worthy of the name have been based upon this thought.

No. No he did not. If it's not clear, no, it's untrue that "all philosophies worthy of the name have been based upon" Kantian objectivism. *No*. Bad WWA.

He also continues his weird schtick of talking about Spinoza's ideas without ever mentioning him or using his terminology. I don't know why, but my guess is that he can't risk telling the reader where he's getting what little philosophy he's actually using, because they'll go read it and find out this book is garbage in comparison.

Notably, too, Spinoza eventually uses his pantheism to claim that people necessarily must be altruistic, and that governments must take care of their citizens, based on his earlier claims. A long one, but I want you to observe the bullshitting happening. This is another long passage where he just repeats himself, to try to make his point sound convincing. Then he caps it off by saying, "oh, and *obviously* there's underlying fixed reality to it all."

...nothing manifests in the effect, unless it is in the cause...

See, look through it. The verb "manifests" is the hinge point. I can point out, for example, that platinum acts as a catalyst for the production of sulfuric acid and no platinum is present in the acid thereby produced, so it was a cause that did not "manifest" in the effect.

WWA's response would presumably be that it manifests through the presence of the acid at all, that it's "present" in causing the reaction in the first place. Because "manifest" is a weasel word, it means whatever he needs it to mean.

It actually means "clearly revealed to the eye or the understanding, open to view or comprehension", and comes from a Latin word that meant, basically, direct evidence used in the legal system (EtymOnline).

So the platinum isn't present, so it therefore did not "manifest," as it's not fucking present.
